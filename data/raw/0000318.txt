								<p>It's surprisingly easy to forget about Led Zeppelin in 2014. With arena rock gradually fading from relevance, or at least being subverted and co-opted as a genre and idea â the closest thing to a stadium-geared blockbuster in the last year might have been One Direction's&nbsp;<em>Midnight Memories</em> â Zeppelin's enduring influence as the ideal of Big Rock Music is less relevant than ever. Even <em>Almost Famous</em>, the Zep-soaked Cameron Crowe flick that helped expose a new generation to the classic rock of their parents, is almost 15 years old at this point. The next time you hear "Stairway to Heaven," it might actually sound interesting and refreshing â which is kinda cool, but just feels intuitively <em>wrong</em>.</p><p>It takes an event like this year's spate of deluxe Zeppelin reissues â the first three were released back in June, while&nbsp;<em>IV</em> and <em>Houses of the Holy</em> are on deck for next month â to remind you of how incredible this band really was. Some elements of the Zeppelin experience haven't dated quite so brilliantly â namely the rampant misogyny, the even more rampant Tolkien references, and the always-terrible 1976 <em>Presence </em>LP â but the songs, the sound, and the sheer <em>size</em> of the group continue to dazzle to this day. If they make music on this scale in 2014, it's certainly not to be found in the rock genre, and you'd be pretty hard-pressed to find it anywhere else, either.</p><p>As a tribute to Led Zeppelin, we've ranked all 87 songs in their recorded catalog. Any song to ever appear on a commercial Led Zeppelin release â even the live ones and the reissues â can be found here; for songs with multiple recorded versions, we opted for the best-known rendition. Enjoy our list, and next time you're in your car or your shower, maybe give the local classic rock station a little love.</p><p><strong>87. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y2hZ6OytDxo">"LA Drone"</a>&nbsp;(<em>How th</em><em>e West Was Won</em>, 2003)</strong><br />A 15-second intro track to the band's excellent live album, this one is true to its name â and included here for strict completionist purposes only.</p><p><strong>86. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B5t8hxyqELw">"Candy Store Rock"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Presence</em>, 1976)<br /></strong>A hookless, aimless, funk-less tribute to Bo Diddley and '50s rock that was somehow released as a single A-side, demonstrating just how out of touch the band was in 1976.</p><p><strong>85. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r1wetZqyVyg">"Darlene"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Coda</em>, 1982)</strong><br />Near-comic in its repititiveness, "Darlene" does eventually build a certain swing to it, but only the most devoted of fans are likely to bear with it long enough to get there.</p><p><strong>84. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Er_nY0v_TB4">"Somethin' Else"</a>&nbsp;(<em>BBC Sessions</em>, 1997)</strong><br />A faithful but muddled-sounding and mostly uninspired Eddie Cochran live cover, showcasing Zep as the unpretentious rock purists they never were (and never should have been).&nbsp;</p><p><strong>83. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hJ6WXssV5c0">"Hot Dog"</a>&nbsp;(<em>In Through the Out Door</em>, 1979)</strong><br />After the untenably monochromatic <em>Presence</em>, Zeppelin decided to go the other way with the disorienting genre-hopping of <em>In Through the Out Door</em>. Several of the experiments were actually quite successful; this cartoonish hoedown excursion was less so.</p><p><strong>82. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FdEKSDG6l9Q">"Royal Orleans"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Presence</em>, 1976)</strong><br />This one's really strong for the first 11 seconds. Unfortunately, they're followed by about 177 seconds of thoughtless funk noodling and in-jokey mewling from Robert Plant.</p><p><strong>81. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gaTDapvHcoY">"Tea For One"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Presence</em>, 1976)</strong><br />A good song â unfortunately, it's one they'd already written six years prior. Jimmy Page has essentially admitted the song was a total rewrite of <em>III</em>'s "Since I've Been Loving You," and Plant sounds so bored with the rehash that he might fall asleep before the two-minute mark.</p><p><strong>80. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xvsrXa2Vy3c">"Ozone Baby"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Coda</em>, 1982)</strong><br />There's a spark there at the beginning, but it's not strong enough to survive the 50th "<em>Ooo-ooh</em> / It's my love" exhortation in the song's quickly-grating chorus.</p><p><img src="/sites/all/files/styles/style620_413/public/led-zeppelin-2_0.jpg" title="Photo by Getty Images" width="620" height="413" class="image-style620_413 editItemMedia editItemPhoto" data-fid="186774" data-larger-src="/sites/all/files/led-zeppelin-2_0.jpg" /></p><p><strong>79. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o5tM4KgXCYQ">"South Bound Saurez"</a>&nbsp;(<em>In Through the Out Door</em>, 1979)</strong><br />More of <em>ITtOD</em>'s self-conscious genre-hopping, this time a jaunty honky-tonk number w<span style="font-size: 14px;">ith Elton John saloon-style piano and one of Plant's more irritating vocals. Someone should've spell-checked that title, too.</span></p><p><strong>78. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ktePjeHtp-4">"Black Country Woman"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Physical Graffiti</em>, 1975)</strong><br />Possibly the slightest song to appear within Zep's original classic six-album run, the throwaway folk jam "Woman" is buried deep enough in side four of the&nbsp;<em>Physical Graffiti</em> double-LP&nbsp;that maybe they hoped no one would notice it. They were mostly right. <strong><br /></strong></p><p><strong>77. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r_JCEcHZZHs">"For Your Life"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Presence</em>,</strong>&nbsp;<strong>1976)</strong><br />Another solid intro in search of a song to follow: Like most of the somewhat ironically titled <em>Presence</em>, it loses itself somewhere in the second or third minute of momentum-less grooving.</p><p><strong>76. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=C5arxGd_GPQ">"You Shook Me"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Led Zeppelin I</em>, 1969)</strong><br />The band's least-successful early blues reinterpretation, aiming for slow and seductive, but overshooting the mark and ending up just kind of sluggish.</p><p><strong>75. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mTZZW0KMgm4">"The Girl I Love She Got Long Black Wavy Hair"</a>&nbsp;(<em>BBC Sessions</em>, 1997)</strong><br />An utterly fantastic title, and if you remember a single other thing about the song, you're a better fan than I.</p><p><strong>74. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II0eIUsNELY">"La La"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Led Zeppelin II (2014 Deluxe Edition Reissue)</em>, 2014)</strong><br />Not a fully-formed song by any means, but a perfectly pleasant organ-led trifle from the first batch of Zep reissues.</p><p><strong>73. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gvKGM93yoCI">"Boogie With Stu"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Physical Graffiti</em>, 1975)</strong><br />Like "Black Country Woman," this is back-end filler for&nbsp;<em>Graffiti. </em>Supremely silly, though there is some legitimate boogieing to be had, at least.</p><p><strong>72. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_jLYWcTzTIo">"Walter's Walk"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Coda</em>, 1982)</strong><br />Hardly one of the group's most memorable rockers, but John Bonham's relentless bass drum-pounding gives the song a pulse that makes it engaging enough. Parts of it may have inspired the White Stripes' <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5roz5-wdjBg">"Icky Thump,"</a>&nbsp;though you could say that about at least four or five Zeppelin songs.</p><p><strong>71. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y94XT94PSdg">"Hots on For Nowhere"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Presence</em>, 1976)</strong><br />Another largely stuck-in-the-muck <em>Presence</em> jam, though this one at least has a catchy hook or two ("La la"s and "Oh-oh"s! Stereophonic double-tracked guitars!) to get you through the near-five-minute runtime.</p><p><strong>70. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nFb9cruqoaY">"Bonzo's Montreux"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Coda</em>, 1982)</strong><br />The musical history of primarily drums-only rock tracks is not a particularly long or engaging one, and there's a reason for that. If you were gonna afford the listening opportunity to one guy, though, Bonzo's not a bad choice; if it were a minute or two shorter it'd be a great mixtape interlude.</p><p><strong>69. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ffkC4vogqcY">"We're Gonna Groove"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Coda</em>, 1982)</strong><br />One of the rawer Zeppelin cuts, and the lead track on the odds-and-sods collection <em>Coda</em>. Not particularly long on hooks, but high on grit and short on runtime, so an enjoyable enough&nbsp;<em>LZII</em> outtake.</p><p><img src="/sites/all/files/styles/style620_413/public/led-zeppelin-3.jpg" title="Photo by Getty Images" width="620" height="413" style="font-size: 13.63636302948px;" class="image-style620_413 editItemMedia editItemPhoto" data-fid="186771" data-larger-src="/sites/all/files/led-zeppelin-3.jpg" /></p><p><strong>68. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0i72LjAnhlg">"Night Flight"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Physical Graffiti</em>, 1975)</strong><br />Interesting as one of the few Zep songs that begins <em>in media res</em>&nbsp;â the band usually makes such a big deal out of their intros, so to hear Plant jumping in straight away ("I received a message from my brother...") is pretty jarring â and as one of the few not to feature a guitar solo. Not interesting for a ton else.</p><p><strong>67. <a href="https://www.google.com/url?q=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.youtube.com%2Fwatch%3Fv%3DMhrLVTl2pxU">"Hats Off to (Roy) Harper"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Led Zeppelin III, </em>1970)</strong><br />One of the oddest songs in the band's discography, right down to the weird use of parentheses in the title. There's not too much of a song there between Plant's uninteligibly vibrato'd vocals and Page's freeform slide guitar, but it's an endearingly idiosyncratic way to end <em>III</em>, and hopefully <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZjSEVSvDkhw">Roy himself</a> found it appropriately flattering.</p><p><strong>66. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UEocukvkkSM">"Baby Come on Home"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Boxed Set 2</em>, 1993)</strong><br />A sort of lost gem of Zeppelin's an Otis Redding-style, organ-drenched soul ballad from the band's pre-LP days as the "New Yardbirds." It sounds convincing enough that it's hard to believe that it's not a cover, and even though it doesn't particularly play to the band's strengths (except for Plant, anyway, who's having an absolute blast), it's a bummer we didn't get to hear more of this side of the band on their albums.</p><p><strong>65. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4w3pOMLVTSY">"How Many More Times"</a>&nbsp;(<em>Led Zeppelin I</em>, 1969)</strong><br />Primarily remembered for John Paul Jones' "Green Onions"-like walking bass line, and the way the intro unfolds from there. His playing in that section, sans accompaniment â rivaled only by <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sI7XiJgt0vY">"Eruption"</a>&nbsp;as the most famous <em>true</em> guitar solo in rock history â is peerlessly electrifying, and the moment when the band kicks back in is nearly as good.</p><p>Taken in full, "Heartbreaker" is an insane mishmash of questionable ideas â hell, how many other songs can you think of that end mid-word? â but the band's brilliance and sheer bravado carries it, making it one of their best and best-remembered songs, and proof that they could simply do things other bands could not.</p><p><span class="spin-video-embed-swap" data-embed-html="&lt;iframe src=&quot;//www.youtube.com/embed/qvI4ll_59SQ?autoplay=1&quot; frameborder=&quot;0&quot; width=&quot;620&quot; height=&quot;349&quot;&gt;&lt;/iframe&gt;" data-placeholder-html="&lt;span class=&quot;spin-vesp&quot;&gt;

	&lt;/span&gt;
&lt;/span&gt;

</span>
</span>
</p><p><strong>2. "Kashmir" (<em>Physical Graffiti</em>, 1975)</strong><br />The song Led Zeppelin themselves would most like you to remember them by, and for good reason. The biggest song on their biggest album in the biggest stretch of their career, "Kashmir" was, obviously, Led Zeppelin's ultimate too-big-to-fail moment: a plodding eight-and-a-half minute journey through a faraway land that Zeppelin themselves had never even been to, a song which was either going to define them as pretentious fops whose reach far exceeded their grasp, or simply, the greatest hard-rock band in the history of recorded music.</p><p>Naturally, it did the latter, as the song stands as their most singular, hypnotic and awesome-in-the-truest-sense epic of the band's career â though they'd have their moments in the former category soon enough. Puff Daddy caught some heat when he sampled the song for the silly "<a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EFefqsm_rRQ">Come With Me</a>" off of the 1998 <em>Godzilla</em> sountrack, but his instincts were right: The "Kashmir" riff is the sound of a gigantic green lizard wrecking a downtown metropolis, and like the rest of the song, it never ceases to amaze.</p><p><span class="spin-video-embed-swap" data-embed-html="&lt;iframe src=&quot;//www.youtube.com/embed/nu6ht1CwZO0?autoplay=1&quot; frameborder=&quot;0&quot; width=&quot;620&quot; height=&quot;349&quot;&gt;&lt;/iframe&gt;" data-placeholder-html="&lt;span class=&quot;spin-vesp&quot;&gt;

</span>
</p><p><strong>1. "Over the Hills and Far Away" (<em>Houses of the Holy</em>, 1973)</strong><br />Why "Over the Hills"? It's not the band's best-known song or biggest hit. It's not the band's most rocking or prettiest song. It's probably not the first song that anyone thinks of when they think of Led Zeppelin. But it <em>is</em> the song that best demonstrates just about everything the band does well: the unforgettable and impossible-to-pin-down opening riff, the life-affirming transition from acoustic to electric, the constant switches in tone and dynamic, the piercing solo with double-tracked climax, the impeccable interplay of guitar, bass, and drum, the inimitable Plant shrieking, the gorgeous coda, even the super-oblique title... it's Zep through and through, checking all of the boxes and kicking your ass while doing so.</p><p>But the thing that really seals it for "Over the Hills" is the sense of wonder it inspires. Zeppelin's greatest quality, apart from the weird time signatures and otherworldly instrumentation and teenage-male-pandering lyrics, was their ability to elevate, to make you believe that there was a secret world of higher musical understanding that only they as the Ultimate Rock Gods had access to, and which they could transport you to for three to ten minutes at a time, depending on which side of which album you were listening to.</p><p>The climax of "Over the Hills," as the song's main hook starts to fold in on itself, and Plant does his "You really ought to know..." wailing, as the song echoes on and on into infinity, is as wondrous as the band ever got, achieving a classic-rock nirvana that only a handful of songs in history have ever been lucky enough to be able to touch. The harpsichord outro and brief fade-in of the rest of the band that closes the song brings it back down to earth a little, but the sensation lingers on far after you're done listening. <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NFgKVGPJmtI">Zeppelin rules</a>.</p><p><span class="spin-video-embed-swap" data-embed-html="&lt;iframe src=&quot;//www.youtube.com/embed/6bD9t44JUD4?autoplay=1&quot; frameborder=&quot;0&quot; width=&quot;620&quot; height=&quot;349&quot;&gt;&lt;/iframe&gt;" data-placeholder-html="&lt;span class=&quot;spin-vesp&quot;&gt;
